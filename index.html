Down-Regulation
Calming the nervous system
Diaphragmatic 4/6 Breathing
▼

A slow, controlled breathing pattern that emphasises a longer exhale to shift the body into a calmer, recovery-focused state.

How to do it
  • Inhale through the nose for 4 seconds
  • Exhale gently for 6 seconds
  • Belly expands on inhale, ribs soften on exhale
  • Keep shoulders relaxed and jaw soft
The science

The diaphragm is directly connected to the vagus nerve. A longer exhale increases vagal activity, slowing heart rate and reducing sympathetic drive. Slow breathing reduces chemoreceptor sensitivity and improves ventilation efficiency without increasing metabolic demand — ideal for low-energy states.

Why it's prescribed
  • Helps regulate autonomic swings common in CFS/ME
  • Reduces tension and improves pacing
  • Supports recovery after exertion
  • Very low cognitive and physical load
Humming Breath
▼

A vibratory exhale that stimulates the vagus nerve and promotes relaxation.

How to do it
  • Inhale softly
  • Exhale with a gentle "mmm" hum
  • Keep the jaw loose and lips lightly closed
  • Feel vibration in the face, throat, and chest
The science

Humming increases nitric oxide production in the nasal passages, improving airflow and oxygen uptake. Vibrations stimulate mechanoreceptors connected to the vagus nerve, reducing amygdala activity and increasing parasympathetic tone.

Why it's prescribed
  • Excellent for down-regulation after exertion
  • Helps with sensory overload and emotional tension
  • Supports sleep and PEM recovery
3/5 Downshift Breath
▼

A quick, accessible calming breath used to 'shift down' the nervous system.

How to do it
  • Inhale for 3 seconds
  • Exhale for 5 seconds
  • Keep breath soft and effortless
The science

Shorter inhale and longer exhale reduces sympathetic firing and helps regulate baroreflex sensitivity, stabilising blood pressure. Useful for rapid autonomic resets without fatigue.

Why it's prescribed
  • Perfect between tasks or transitions
  • Helps avoid autonomic spikes
  • Very low effort for CFS/ME
Soft Sigh Exhale
▼

A natural tension-release breath that resets the breathing rhythm.

How to do it
  • Inhale normally
  • Exhale with a soft sigh
  • Let shoulders drop naturally
The science

Sighing resets the pre-Bötzinger complex, the brainstem's breathing rhythm generator. Reduces upper-trap hypertonicity via reflex inhibition and helps release chronic bracing patterns in the neck and shoulders.

Why it's prescribed
  • Ideal for desk-based tension
  • Helps reduce shoulder/neck guarding
  • Supports emotional regulation
Coherence Breathing (5/5)
▼

Breathing at ~6 breaths/min to synchronise heart and breath rhythms.

How to do it
  • Inhale for 5 seconds
  • Exhale for 5 seconds
  • Smooth, even rhythm
The science

Creates cardiorespiratory coherence — heart rate and breath rhythm align. Improves HRV, a marker of autonomic flexibility and resilience. Reduces activation of the HPA axis (stress system) and enhances cognitive clarity and emotional stability.

Why it's prescribed
  • Helps stabilise autonomic swings
  • Supports pacing and emotional regulation
  • Great before strength sessions
Resonance Breathing (5.5/5.5)
▼

A precise breathing rate that optimises baroreflex function and HRV.

How to do it
  • Inhale for 5.5 seconds
  • Exhale for 5.5 seconds
  • Maintain nasal breathing
The science

Resonance frequency breathing maximises baroreflex gain, stabilising blood pressure. Reduces orthostatic intolerance symptoms and produces the strongest improvements in HRV of any breathing pattern.

Why it's prescribed
  • Ideal for clients with dizziness or autonomic instability
  • Supports recovery after exertion

  • Drop counting
  • Let breath settle
  • No effort
The science

Prevents over-regulation and supports interoceptive awareness. Helps integrate breathwork into daily life.

Why it's prescribed

Micro Breath Holds (1–2 sec)
▼

Tiny breath holds to improve CO₂ tolerance and reduce breathlessness.

How to do it
  • Exhale
  • Hold 1–2 seconds
  • Inhale gently
The science

Increases tolerance to rising CO₂, reduces breathlessness, and helps stabilise breathing patterns.

Why it's prescribed
  • Useful for clients who over-breathe
  • Only used if tolerated
Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)
▼

A structured breath for focus and calm.

How to do it
  • Inhale 4
  • Hold 4
  • Exhale 4
  • Hold 4
The science

Breath holds increase CO₂ tolerance. Reduces activity in the locus coeruleus, the brain's arousal centre, and improves cognitive control and emotional stability.

Why it's prescribed
  • Great for work stress
  • Helps regulate pacing

How to do it

The science

Reduces sympathetic activation and helps regulate breath rhythm. Low cognitive load makes it ideal for high-overwhelm moments.

Why it's prescribed
  • Ideal for overwhelm
  • Great for transitions

Sleep & Wind-Down
Preparing the body and mind for rest
Modified 4/7/8 (CFS-Safe)
▼

A sleep-focused breath pattern with shortened holds to avoid dizziness.

How to do it
  • Inhale 4
  • Hold 2–3
  • Exhale 6–8
The science

Breath holds increase CO₂ tolerance, reducing hyperventilation tendencies. Long exhale activates parasympathetic pathways. Modified to avoid dizziness in low-energy states.

Why it's prescribed
  • Helps sleep onset
  • Supports evening down-regulation
Extended Exhale Breathing
▼

A sleep-focused breath pattern.

How to do it
  • Inhale 4
  • Exhale 8
The science

Long exhale increases parasympathetic dominance, reduces cortical arousal, and helps transition into sleep.

Why it's prescribed
  • Helps sleep onset
  • Ideal for evening routine
Humming + Body Scan
▼

Combines vagal stimulation with somatic awareness.

How to do it
  • Hum on exhale
  • Scan body from feet to face
The science

Vibration stimulates the vagus nerve while body scanning reduces somatic tension, helping shift into a parasympathetic state.

Why it's prescribed
  • Excellent for sleep
  • Helps reduce full-body tension
3-Part Breath (Dirga)
▼

A layered breath expanding belly → ribs → chest.

How to do it
  • Inhale belly → ribs → chest
  • Exhale chest → ribs → belly
The science

Improves diaphragm excursion and enhances rib mobility. Reduces anxiety and emotional load.

Why it's prescribed
  • Great for emotional regulation
  • Helps with breath awareness

Movement & Exercise
Breathing during and around physical activity
Exhale on Effort
▼

Breathing pattern for strength movements.

How to do it
  • Exhale during lift
  • Inhale during return
The science

Reduces intra-abdominal pressure spikes and prevents breath-holding (Valsalva). Improves stability and reduces dizziness.

Why it's prescribed
  • Safe for CFS/ME
  • Supports pacing during strength work
Nasal Breathing on Walking Pad
▼

Low-effort nasal breathing during walking.

How to do it
  • Mouth closed
  • Slow nasal breaths
The science

Improves oxygen uptake, reduces hyperventilation, and supports aerobic efficiency.

Why it's prescribed
  • Keeps walking low-intensity
  • Helps avoid PEM
Breath-Paced Mobility
▼

Using breath to guide movement.

How to do it
  • Inhale to open
  • Exhale to fold
The science

Breath-movement coupling reduces muscle tension, improves motor control, and enhances parasympathetic activation.

Why it's prescribed
  • Supports mobility without fatigue
  • Helps regulate pacing
Nasal Low-Volume Breathing
▼

Quiet, slow nasal breathing that reduces over-breathing.

How to do it
  • Mouth closed
  • Gentle nasal inhale/exhale
  • Keep breath small and quiet
The science

Nasal breathing increases nitric oxide, improving oxygen uptake. Reduces respiratory alkalosis caused by over-breathing and supports efficient gas exchange.

Why it's prescribed
  • Ideal during walking pad
  • Helps regulate breathing during movement

Structural & Postural Breathing
Improving mechanics and rib mobility
Lateral Rib Breathing (Left-Side Focus)
▼

Breathing into the side ribs to improve expansion and correct asymmetry.

How to do it
  • Lie on right side
  • Place hand on left ribs
  • Inhale into the side of the ribcage
  • Exhale long and soft
The science

Improves rib mobility and diaphragm excursion. Helps correct left-right asymmetry in breathing mechanics, enhances sensory input to the left side, and supports thoracic rotation and scapular mechanics.

Why it's prescribed
  • Supports left-side neuromuscular activation
  • Helps reduce compensatory patterns
Thoracic Expansion Breathing
▼

Breathing into the upper ribs and mid-back to improve mobility.

How to do it
  • Hands on ribs
  • Inhale → ribs widen
  • Exhale → ribs soften
The science

Improves thoracic mobility and rib expansion. Reduces accessory muscle overuse in the neck and shoulders. Supports scapular mechanics and shoulder rehab.

Why it's prescribed
  • Helps shoulder rehab
  • Reduces neck tension
